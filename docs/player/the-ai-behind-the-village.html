The AI behind the village

What's actually "thinking" for your villagers, what's just habit, and why some decisions use a fast cheap model while others use a slower, better one.

Nature and soul: what stays fixed, what grows

Every villager starts with a written personality — their nature — set once when the world is created and never changed again for the rest of that villager's life. On top of that fixed nature, each villager accumulates a "soul": a growing record of memories, beliefs, and a sense of who they're becoming, shaped by everything that happens to them. The short version: a villager's souls change over time, but their underlying nature doesn't. That split is enforced, not just a design intention — see below.

Not everything needs an AI call

Plenty of what a villager does — walking somewhere, eating, sleeping, basic reactions — happens instantly through simple built-in rules, no AI model involved at all. Bigger moments — deciding what to do next, how to respond in a conversation, what to say — go through an actual AI model "thinking" as that villager, using their persona and recent memories as the basis for the decision. This mix is why a village can run smoothly at high speed instead of grinding to a halt waiting on AI responses for every little thing: most of a villager's second-to-second existence is free, and AI is spent where it actually matters.

Local model vs. cloud model

promptworld can split AI work across two kinds of models: a fast, typically free model running on your own computer for high-volume, routine decisions (like the everyday choices villagers make while going about their day), and a slower but higher-quality cloud model reserved for the handful of things worth paying real quality for — most notably nightly memory digestion (below) and the narrated chronicle, plus Metatron's own conversation with you. This split is configurable, and the setup basics page walks through the minimum you need to turn it on.

Why villagers sometimes feel more "on autopilot" at very high speed

An AI model takes real wall-clock time to respond, but the game world keeps ticking during that time. At very high game speeds, a slow decision risks arriving too late to still make sense for the moment it was meant for. Rather than let a stale decision land anyway, the game simply falls back to the simple built-in behavior for that moment instead. You may notice villagers acting a bit more routine-driven at the fastest speeds — that's this trade-off in action, not a bug; dial the speed back down and full AI-driven decisions resume.

Sleep is when a soul actually grows

Once a night, when a villager sleeps, one AI call (using the higher-quality cloud model) reviews everything memorable that happened to them that day and folds it into their permanent soul: some memories get promoted as worth keeping long-term, some fade away, new beliefs form (each one noting how sure the villager is, and whether they witnessed it themselves, were told, or simply inferred it), and their short self-narrative — "who I am becoming" — gets updated. Crucially, this process is not allowed to touch the villager's underlying nature: a separate, purely mechanical check throws out any digestion attempt that starts drifting the villager's fundamental temperament. The nightly digest can only grow the soul; it can never rewrite the nature underneath it.

Relationships, gossip, and grudges

Villagers keep track of how they feel about each other — trust and fondness that rise and fall based on what actually happens between them: talking, giving gifts, breaking a promise, or taking from someone's storage chest without asking (which is noticed and remembered, and damages the relationship, even though the goods still change hands). Rumors spread from villager to villager, getting slightly reworded each time they're retold, and a villager's confidence in a rumor fades the more times it's been passed along — exactly how gossip behaves in real life. None of this is decoration: real grudges and real friendships form from it, and you can read the results — arguments, favors, broken promises — right in the chronicle.
