The AI behind the village

What's actually "thinking" for your villagers, what's just habit, and why some decisions use a fast cheap model while others use a slower, better one.

Nature and soul: what stays fixed, what grows

Every villager starts with a written personality — their nature — set once when the world is created and never changed again for the rest of that villager's life. On top of that fixed nature, each villager accumulates a "soul": a growing record of memories, beliefs, and a sense of who they're becoming, shaped by everything that happens to them. The short version: a villager's souls change over time, but their underlying nature doesn't. That split is enforced, not just a design intention — see below.

Not everything needs an AI call

Plenty of what a villager does — walking somewhere, eating, sleeping, basic reactions — happens instantly through simple built-in rules, no AI model involved at all. Bigger moments — deciding what to do next, how to respond in a conversation, what to say — go through an actual AI model "thinking" as that villager, using their persona and recent memories as the basis for the decision. This mix is why a village can run smoothly at high speed instead of grinding to a halt waiting on AI responses for every little thing: most of a villager's second-to-second existence is free, and AI is spent where it actually matters.

One thought, one act

When a villager does stop to think, the AI is told three things, plainly: who this villager is (one line, plus their written nature), what their situation looks like right now, and what they remember. Then it must commit to exactly one action for that moment — do something in the world, set a short plan, or just muse. Musing counts: a moment spent thinking is a moment not spent doing, so even daydreaming is a real choice with a real cost, not a free extra. That "one thought, one act" rule is built into the game's machinery, so a villager can never act twice off a single thought.

The exact wording villagers are given got a careful rewrite recently — and because this is a game about prompt-writing, the rewrite was held to its own standard: the old and new versions were run head-to-head in identical worlds and only the measurably better one shipped. The new wording produces noticeably fewer garbled decisions, and villagers spend about half as much time lost in thought and more time actually doing things. (A version that also included a worked example was tested too — and rejected, because villagers started copying the example instead of thinking for themselves.)

Local model vs. cloud model

promptworld can split AI work across two kinds of models: a fast, typically free model running on your own computer for high-volume, routine decisions (like the everyday choices villagers make while going about their day), and a slower but higher-quality cloud model reserved for the handful of things worth paying real quality for — most notably nightly memory digestion (below) and the narrated chronicle, plus Metatron's own conversation with you. This split is configurable, and the setup basics page walks through the minimum you need to turn it on.

There's a third, much smaller kind of AI call too: when you've asked Metatron to watch for something loosely phrased ("when Rowan seems heartbroken"), confirming that a matching moment really fits what you described takes one tiny, cheap yes/no check — far smaller than a real conversational reply, and rate-limited so it can never turn watching into meaningful spend. By default it tries the local model first and only asks the cloud model if that isn't available. If you're running an older world whose AI configuration predates this feature, promptworld quietly fills in a sensible default for it the next time the world starts, and says so in one line in the startup log — nothing you need to do by hand.

Why villagers sometimes feel more "on autopilot" at very high speed

An AI model takes real wall-clock time to respond, but the game world keeps ticking during that time. At very high game speeds, a slow decision risks arriving too late to still make sense for the moment it was meant for. Rather than let a stale decision land anyway, the game simply falls back to the simple built-in behavior for that moment instead. You may notice villagers acting a bit more routine-driven at the fastest speeds — that's this trade-off in action, not a bug; dial the speed back down and full AI-driven decisions resume.

The game keeps relearning how slow its own AI actually is

Behind the "is this decision still worth waiting for" call above, the game keeps a running sense of how long a typical thought actually takes to come back from whatever AI model is answering it. That pace isn't assumed to be fixed forever: if a model starts consistently taking a lot longer than it used to — busier hardware, a different model swapped in, or anything else — the game notices once enough recent thoughts have run well over its expectation, and it updates its own sense of "how long this normally takes" to match what's actually happening now, instead of clinging to an outdated number. That keeps every speed and staleness decision honest — grounded in how the AI is actually performing right now, not how it used to perform a while back.

The world would rather slow down than let a thought go stale

The autopilot fallback above is a last resort, not the first line of defense. Underneath it, the world keeps a running measure of how many villager thoughts are currently in flight and how overdue they're getting for the speed you asked for. A thought that's already running late counts for exactly how late it is — the longer it drags on, the more it weighs — so a village that's genuinely struggling to keep up always gets caught by this and slowed down, rather than slipping through unnoticed. If that overdue-ness climbs too high for a few seconds running, the world quietly drops your effective speed by one notch on its own — giving whatever model is serving your villagers more real time to think per game-second, no input needed from you. Once the backlog clears for a good stretch, it climbs back up again, all the way to the speed you actually asked for. Your own speed and pause commands always cut through this instantly. See the time & speed page for what this looks like in the full-screen client's header and chronicle feed.

The two mechanisms work together, in order: the world tries slowing itself down first, buying villagers more real thinking time; only if you're already down at the slowest speed on the watchable ladder and villagers are still falling behind does the autopilot fallback above kick in.

Sleep is when a soul actually grows

Once a night, when a villager sleeps, one AI call (using the higher-quality cloud model) reviews everything memorable that happened to them that day and folds it into their permanent soul: some memories get promoted as worth keeping long-term, some fade away, new beliefs form (each one noting how sure the villager is, and whether they witnessed it themselves, were told, or simply inferred it), and their short self-narrative — "who I am becoming" — gets updated. Crucially, this process is not allowed to touch the villager's underlying nature: a separate, purely mechanical check throws out any digestion attempt that starts drifting the villager's fundamental temperament. The nightly digest can only grow the soul; it can never rewrite the nature underneath it.

The game doesn't just take a villager's word for "I witnessed this." Every memory quietly carries a record of where it actually came from — something the villager did or saw themselves, versus something they only heard about, inferred, or read as a one-line summary of a longer conversation. When a new belief claims to be "witnessed," the game checks that claim against that record: if it isn't actually backed by something the villager directly experienced, the claim is honestly downgraded to "told" or "inferred" instead, even if the villager's own account overstated it. Nobody in your village can brag about having seen something they only heard secondhand.

Beliefs fade into half-remembered myth if nothing keeps them alive

A belief doesn't stay at full strength forever just because a villager once formed it. Its confidence quietly fades the longer it goes unconfirmed — roughly halving every eight game days, far slower than an ordinary memory (which fades within about a day), so a real conviction still outlasts a fleeting impression. Once a belief's confidence has faded far enough, it stops steering how that villager behaves, and you'll see it rendered in their soul not as a stated fact but as a hedged "half-remembered: ..." line instead. Nothing needs to happen for this to occur — it's simply what happens to an old belief nobody has re-confirmed. A belief a villager keeps re-experiencing or re-confirming, on the other hand, stays fresh and never fades.

Relationships, gossip, and grudges

Villagers keep track of how they feel about each other — trust and fondness that rise and fall based on what actually happens between them: talking, giving gifts, breaking a promise, or taking from someone's storage chest without asking (which is noticed and remembered, and damages the relationship, even though the goods still change hands). Rumors spread from villager to villager, getting slightly reworded each time they're retold, and a villager's confidence in a rumor fades the more times it's been passed along — exactly how gossip behaves in real life. None of this is decoration: real grudges and real friendships form from it, and you can read the results — arguments, favors, broken promises — right in the chronicle.
