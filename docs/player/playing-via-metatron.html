Playing via Metatron

You never touch a villager directly. Everything you do to the village goes through one character: Metatron, the angel who watches the world and mediates your influence.

Metatron is your only verb

Your words as a player have exactly one destination: Metatron's own prompt. Villagers never see what you type — they can only ever receive whatever Metatron decides to pass along, and only in the shaped forms described below. This is deliberate: Metatron is a gatekeeper, not a megaphone. You talk to it (in the console, or the Metatron pane of the full-screen client), it watches the village, and it decides what — if anything — to do about what you asked.

Shaping your angel: the charter

The real meta-game is prompt-engineering Metatron itself. At the root of your world's save directory sits charter.md — a plain text file, seeded when the world is created and never overwritten by the game, that you can edit anytime. It's read fresh every single turn, so an edit you make takes effect on Metatron's very next reply. Think of it like giving your angel a personality and standing instructions: sarcastic, protective, meddling, hands-off — whatever tone and priorities you write in there is who Metatron becomes.

You can also add narrower instruction files in a skills/ folder next to the charter, and control exactly which abilities Metatron is allowed to use — visions, omens, standing orders, miracles, and clock control alike — via a capabilities.json file. A world with no such file grants the full set of abilities by default.

Underneath anything you write, two rules always hold no matter what the charter says: Metatron will never invent an event it didn't actually observe, and it will never pass your literal words straight to a villager. Those two guardrails aren't editable — everything else about its voice and judgment is yours to shape. A related rule binds the abilities below: Metatron only acts when you ask it to, or when a standing order you placed authorizes it — never on its own initiative.

Reaching a villager's mind: visions and omens

When Metatron decides to plant something in a villager's mind — rather than changing the world directly — it has two forms, spent from the same bank of "charges" described below:

A vision — one villager, any time

A vision reaches exactly one living villager, at any hour of the day or night. It lands as a memory the villager will interpret in their own persona — a vision doesn't force a villager to do anything, it just gives them something to react to, like anyone would react to a strange or striking dream.

An omen — one villager, a named few, or everyone, but only after dark

An omen can reach one villager, a comma-separated handful of named villagers, or the whole village at once ("everyone") — but only at night. If you ask for an omen during the day, Metatron doesn't refuse and doesn't lose the request: it quietly holds onto it and sends the very same omen automatically the moment night falls, to whoever is still alive by then. Either way, landing an omen costs exactly one charge, no matter how many villagers it reaches.

Standing orders: tell it once, and walk away

You can also give Metatron a standing instruction — "when Rowan next falls asleep, send her a comforting vision" — and simply walk away. The angel keeps watching for that condition for free; it only spends a charge if and when the watched-for moment actually arrives and it acts on your behalf.

  • Up to 3 of your own standing orders can be active at once.
  • Each one expires on its own after a few game days if it never fires (you can choose a shorter or longer window when you place it).
  • You can ask Metatron what orders currently stand, and cancel any of them, any time.
  • When an order fires, Metatron acts through all of its normal rules — the same charter, the same guardrails, the same charge economy as anything you ask for directly — and tells you what it did the next time you open the console, even if that's days later.
  • If Metatron's charges (or its model budget) are exhausted right when an order fires, it doesn't fake the act — it honestly tells you it couldn't follow through, rather than pretending something happened that didn't.

A watch phrased loosely — "when Rowan seems heartbroken," rather than a plain named event — costs Metatron a small, cheap, rate-limited extra check each time something plausibly matches, just to confirm the fuzzy condition is really true before it acts on it. This never draws from your charge bank; only a landed vision, omen, or miracle does that.

Asking for pause, resume, or a speed change

You can also just ask Metatron in conversation to pause the world, start it back up (at a given speed, if you like), or change its speed — the same controls available from the command line or the full-screen client. This costs nothing, and Metatron will only ever touch the clock when you ask directly, or when a standing order you placed specifically authorizes it — never on its own initiative.

Miracles — direct edits to the world

A miracle is a concrete, direct change to the world itself, of which there are exactly four kinds:

MiracleWhat it does
Snap timeJump the world's clock forward to a specific time (forward only — you can't rewind)
Give an itemHand a chosen villager some quantity of an item
Move somethingRelocate a villager, a structure, or a pile of goods to a new spot
Remove somethingDelete a structure, a pile, or a patch of terrain — villagers themselves can never be removed by any miracle, ever

Miracles are memorable too — a time snap feels to every villager like time lurching forward in an instant; a moved villager feels like an unseen hand lifted them somewhere new; a given item just appears in the recipient's hands "as if set there by an unseen giver."

The charge economy

You start with 1 charge, the bank caps at 3, and it regenerates by 1 every 6 game hours. A landed vision or omen costs 1 charge regardless of who it reaches; most miracles cost 1 charge too, with snapping time the priciest move at 2 charges. Run out, and Metatron will politely decline and explain why — that refusal costs nothing, so you can always keep talking even with an empty bank. Placing a standing order, and Metatron simply watching for it, are both free; only the eventual landed act spends a charge.

The village's own laws (not Metatron's business)

Separately from anything you do, the village governs itself: once a day the villagers gather at a meeting, air grievances, and vote new rules (like a curfew, or banishing someone) into a standing charter of their own — village_charter.md, distinct from your charter.md. Whether a villager actually obeys a law is up to their own persona; the rules aren't force fields, they're social pressure with real, witnessed consequences when broken. You can't vote or propose rules yourself, but the chronicle narrates the assemblies, tallies, and any exiles as they happen, so you always know what your village has decided for itself.
