Three ways to build script-world

Same premise — a terminal, a procedurally generated map, 10–20 AI-programmable agents — but three different games, each built on a different answer to the design question that decides everything else: what is a prompt? A conversation you keep having, a personality you release into a society, or a program you seal and ship. Mechanics are the point of this document; every screen sketch is a placeholder.

Update 2026-07-18: a direction was chosen (decision-1 on the board) — Rumor Mill wins, hybridized with fixed personas, growing souls, and nudge-only influence. See Chosen direction; the three concepts below stand as the record they were chosen from.

Decision · 2026-07-18 · decision-1 on the board

Chosen direction: Rumor Mill, fixed souls

Cast twenty souls. Let them live and grow. Influence them only by suggestion.

The four rules

  1. ~20 agents at world seed (up from the concept's 12).
  2. Persona + soul: each agent starts from a specific authored personality prompt, paired with a dynamic meta-layer — a soul.md of memories, relationships, beliefs. Agents live; experience accretes into the soul.
  3. Personas are fixed. Direct modification is not a player verb — the original pitch's "recast" is removed.
  4. All influence is indirect: suggestions and nudges à la the rumor mechanics. You can nudge; you cannot edit.

Persona / soul split

  • persona.md — immutable, authored once at seed. Temperament, values, fears, voice, one secret. The character's DNA; it never enters any write path — not the player's, not the simulation's.
  • soul.md — the agent's living record, written only by the sim. Episodic memories (each with salience and decay), relationship stances, beliefs (each with confidence and provenance), and a self-narrative in the agent's own voice. Player-readable — reading souls is a core pleasure — never player-writable.
  • Behavior = fixed persona interpreting the accumulated soul in the current situation. The same nudge lands differently on different agents, and differently on the same agent a season later. That double contingency is the game.
  • Nightly consolidation is where growth lives. At sleep, the day's episodic buffer compresses into the soul: memories promoted or faded, beliefs revised, self-narrative rewritten. One LLM call per agent per night — growth is mechanically real and cost-bounded. Constraint on the consolidator: beliefs and relationships may drift; temperament may not. Souls change; natures don't.

The nudge — the only write channel

  • Forms with tradeoffs: a dream (delivered at sleep — intimate, deniable), an omen (a found detail in the world — public, interpretable by whoever notices), an overheard remark (targets one agent, implicates none). Reach vs. deniability is the choice.
  • A nudge is evidence, not command. It enters the target's episodic memory tagged provenance unknown; the fixed persona decides what it means. Skeptics discount, zealots amplify, schemers exploit. Resistance is characterization, not failure.
  • Scarce by design: a nudge budget with cooldown, so each one is a considered move, not a firehose. (Exact economy: open question for the Q/A.)
  • Second-order play is the real game: the rumor graph carries influence further than any single nudge — pick the right soul as the entry point and let Marta do your work on Fen.
  • The attribution loop: agents explain unexplained thoughts to themselves — intuition, ghosts, gods. Shared attributions can consolidate into village culture or religion: the influence channel itself generating emergent meaning.

New risks this direction takes on

  • A miscast agent can't be fixed — only influenced. The design needs a valve: social pressure, exile by vote, possibly generational turnover (agents age; heirs blend personas). Which valve is a Q/A topic, not a foregone conclusion.
  • 20 souls cost more than 12: consolidation is per-agent-per-night and social encounters scale roughly with density. Tiered models and prompt caching graduate from optimizations to requirements.
  • Soul drift could flatten character — years of consolidation eroding what made an agent themselves. The persona/temperament firewall above is the mitigation; it needs testing, not trust.

Revised MVP slice

10–12 agents (20 is the target, not the proof), the persona/soul split with nightly consolidation, one nudge form (the dream), rumor mutation on retell, and the chronicle. Proves the one thing that matters now: does a nudge, filtered through a fixed persona and a lived soul, produce a legible story you feel you authored?

Concept A · the live terrariummechanics mine

Homestead

The prompt is a conversation. You garden behaviors while the world runs.

kin: RimWorld / Dwarf Fortress, with the zones-and-menus layer replaced by talking to your dwarf

The fantasy

You found a settlement with five strangers and raise it by raising them. Every agent carries a charter — a plain-language prompt you can rewrite at any moment — and the craft of the game is diagnosis: watching an agent do something baffling, reading its journal to learn why, and editing the words that made it act that way. The settlement's shape is a portrait of how you write.

observe the sim → something goes sideways → read the agent's journal → rewrite its charter → unpause → watch the fix ripple ↺

Mechanics

Prompt surface

  • The charter — one persistent prompt per agent (role, priorities, standing rules, personality). Editable anytime; takes effect at the agent's next decision point, not retroactively — agents finish the thought they're in, which makes edits feel like persuasion rather than possession.
  • Standing rules are real triggers. Conditional lines ("if storage drops below 20 meals, cook") are extracted into cheap watchers the sim evaluates every tick — the LLM only wakes when a rule fires. Writing crisp conditions is learnable skill.
  • The journal closes the loop. Each decision logs a one-line reason in the agent's voice. Bad behavior is always traceable to a sentence you wrote — the game's debugger.

Simulation systems

  • Needs: hunger, rest, warmth, morale — classic colony-sim drives that generate pressure the charters must handle.
  • Resource chains: forage→farm→cook, chop→saw→build, shear→spin→clothe. Chains are deliberately multi-agent so charters must reference each other ("haul whatever Wren harvests").
  • World: procgen biomes, seasons, weather; mild threats (storms, wolves, fire, blight) — pressure, not war.
  • Skills grow with use, so a charter is also a curriculum: what you tell an agent to spend time on shapes who they become.

Player verbs

Pause/speed time · edit any charter · inspect journal and needs · pin a map annotation agents can read ("build the barn here") · answer an agent's question (agents may ask you for a ruling when their charter is ambiguous — the game teaching you to write better).

Stakes & progression

Soft-fail: agents don't die, they leave — a settlement that can't feed or dignify people bleeds population. Progression is settlement tier (camp → hamlet → village) plus new arrivals with quirky starting personalities that resist naive charters.

MVP slice

One biome, 5 agents, hunger + wood + food chain, the charter editor, journal, and the decision-point loop. No seasons, no threats, no arrivals. Proves the one thing that matters: does rewriting a sentence visibly change behavior within a minute?

Biggest risk

Latency between edit and visible effect. If the causal chain from your words to the world is slow or murky, the core verb dies. Mitigation: decision points fire immediately on charter edit, and the journal cites the charter line that drove each choice.

Concept B · the story enginechosen basis

Rumor Mill

The prompt is a personality. You cast a village and read what it writes.

kin: Dwarf Fortress legends mode / The Sims / Middle-earth's nemesis systems — society as the simulation

The fantasy

You are not a mayor; you are a casting director and, occasionally, a whisper. You author twelve personalities — grudges, ambitions, secrets — drop them into a procgen village, and the game's output is a chronicle: an evolving, readable saga of alliances, feuds, votes, and betrayals that nobody scripted. The economy and farming still exist, but as fuel for social friction, not as the goal.

cast personalities → society runs → the chronicle surfaces a story thread → nudge it (whisper / recast) → read what happens ↺

Mechanics

Prompt surface

  • The persona — each agent's prompt is written as character, not policy: values, fears, loyalties, a secret. Edits are rare and dramatic — recasting someone mid-saga visibly changes them, and other agents notice the change ("Bel hasn't been herself").
  • The whisper — your main verb. Plant a thought, dream, or omen into one agent's memory. It's evidence, not command: the agent interprets it through its persona, and may ignore, distort, or weaponize it.

Social simulation — the centerpiece

  • Relationship graph: every pair carries trust, affection, and debt. All social acts read and write these edges.
  • Rumors are objects. A rumor has content, source, and confidence; it mutates as it's retold (each hop is a cheap LLM paraphrase filtered through the teller's persona). Provenance is tracked, so lies can be traced — and avenged.
  • Promises and debts are objects too — agents remember who broke what, and reputation is computed from the ledger, not vibes.
  • Norms: agents can propose village rules; votes follow the relationship graph. Passed norms become world constraints agents then obey, skirt, or defy — society legislating itself.
  • The chronicle is a generated narrative log with filters (per-agent, per-feud) — the game's primary "screen," compressed from sim events by a narrator model.

Player verbs

Whisper · recast a persona · read the chronicle · inspect the relationship graph · convene (force a village meeting on a topic) · export a finished saga as text.

Stakes & progression

No fail state — the chronicle is the score. Optional "seasons" end with a historical summary (wars averted, dynasties formed) and unlock persona archetypes. The endgame artifact is the exported saga: a story you provably co-authored.

MVP slice

One village, 8 agents, the relationship graph, rumor objects with mutation-on-retell, the whisper verb, and a chronicle log. No economy, no norms, no votes. Proves the one thing that matters: does a planted rumor produce a story worth retelling?

Biggest risk

Soap-opera mush — LLM agents drift toward agreeable, low-stakes chatter. Mitigation: conflict is mechanical, not stylistic — scarcity, secrets, and the debt ledger force zero-sum situations the personas must resolve in character.

Concept C · the prompt roguelikeshelved

Sealed Orders

The prompt is a program. Write it, seal it, and watch it survive winter — or not.

kin: Screeps / roguelikes / autobattlers — programming game where the language is English

The fantasy

Before the run starts you write your tribe's orders — then they're sealed. For a 90-day procgen year of escalating scarcity, raids, and winter, you cannot intervene: you can only watch, fast-forward, and take notes. Your agents live or die by how robustly you wrote. Then the post-mortem shows you exactly which sentence failed, and you draft better orders for the next seed. It's a roguelike where the thing that levels up is your prose.

draft orders → seal → 90-day run (hands off) → post-mortem replay → revise the prompt library → new seed ↺

Mechanics

Prompt surface

  • Orders — one prompt per agent plus one shared tribe doctrine, all locked at day 0. The writing challenge is anticipation: covering situations you haven't seen, on a map you've only scouted from a pre-run survey report.
  • Attention is an in-game resource. You hold a fixed budget of LLM decisions per day and allocate it across agents before sealing (the scout thinks 6×/day, the keeper 2×). Between decisions agents run on cheap reflexes. Token economics becomes strategy instead of a hidden cost.
  • The post-mortem is the real screen: a scrubbable timeline of every decision with its context and reasoning, diffable against previous runs — the tool that turns losing into learning.

Run structure

  • Pressure curve: procgen year with a survey phase, abundance, a raid season, then winter as the boss. Scoring: survivors, granary surplus, monuments built.
  • Permadeath per run; metaprogression is knowledge — your prompt library, unlocked agent archetypes, and harder biomes. No stat inflation: a day-1 player with great prose can beat a veteran's numbers.
  • Daily seed: everyone gets the same map; scores and (optionally) winning order-sets are shareable text files. Async competition falls out for free — it's a prompt-golf leaderboard.

Player verbs

Draft/revise orders (between runs) · allocate attention · fast-forward and bookmark · post-mortem scrub · manage the prompt library · share/import order-sets and seeds.

Stakes & progression

Hard fail, low cost: a run is 20–40 minutes of watching at speed. The emotional loop is the roguelike one — death is a lesson, and the lesson is always legible because every decision carries its reasoning.

MVP slice

A 30-day run, 6 agents, food + firewood + one winter, sealed orders, attention budget, scoring, and a minimal decision-log replay. No raids, no daily seed. Proves the one thing that matters: is a hands-off run tense to watch, and does the post-mortem make you itch to redraft?

Biggest risk

Watching without touching gets boring if runs are too long or too opaque. Mitigation: aggressive time compression with auto-slowdown on drama (a fire, a death, a rule conflict), and bookmarks so the run is skimmable like a replay, not endured like a stream.

Common foundation

The shared engine

All three concepts sit on the same two-layer agent brain — they differ in when the top layer runs and who may edit it.

Two-layer brain

The sim must tick deterministically many times a second; an LLM cannot be in that loop. So the body is code and the mind is called sparingly:

┌───────────────────────── PLANNER (LLM, sparse) ─────────────────────────┐
│ in:  charter/persona/orders + journal + local world summary             │
│ out: structured intent (goal, reason, steps, until-condition)           │
└──────────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────┘
                     fires on decision points only:
             idle · interrupted · standing rule triggered · world event
┌──────────────────────────────▼──────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ EXECUTOR (deterministic sim, every tick)                                │
│ pathfinding · action steps · needs decay · resource flows · watchers    │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

A planner call returns an intent the executor can run unattended for minutes of game time:

{ "goal":  "restock the granary",
  "why":   "storage 18 < 20 — charter says cook first",
  "steps": [["goto","field_nw"], ["harvest","wheat",12],
            ["goto","kitchen"], ["cook","meals",12]],
  "until": "storage >= 30, or interrupted" }

The why field is load-bearing in all three games: it becomes Homestead's journal, Rumor Mill's chronicle fodder, and Sealed Orders' post-mortem log.

Cost control levers

  • Decision points, not polling — the LLM never runs on a timer.
  • Tiered models — small/fast for routine intents and rumor paraphrases; a larger model for milestone decisions and the narrator.
  • Prompt caching — charters and world-static context are cache-stable prefixes; only the journal tail and local summary vary per call.
  • Sealed Orders goes further: the budget is a visible game mechanic, which also makes it the cheapest and most cost-predictable concept to operate.

Terminal stack

Any modern TUI framework fits the two-layer design — e.g. ratatui (Rust), textual (Python), or ink (Node). The map is a glyph grid with a 16-color-safe palette; panes are the primitive (map / inspector / editor / log). Choice is a spec-phase decision, not a design constraint.

Pre-decision reading — superseded

Homestead is the most direct realization of the README and the safest first build. Sealed Orders is the most mechanically novel and by far the most cost-controlled — its attention budget turns the technology's main liability into a strategy layer. Rumor Mill has the highest ceiling (the chronicle is a shareable artifact) and the highest tuning risk. Superseded 2026-07-18: Rumor Mill won — see the Chosen direction, which hybridizes in Homestead's growth mechanics and Sealed Orders' cost discipline.

Where this goes

Next step

The direction is chosen (decision-1); the board's first task — the Socratic grounding session (TASK-1) — now interrogates the fixed-souls hybrid rather than three open concepts, then feeds a Spec Kit spec (specs/001-…) linked onto the Backlog board. Questions the Q/A must answer:

  • Consolidation contract: exactly what may drift in a soul (beliefs, relationships, self-narrative) and what never may (temperament, values)? How is that firewall enforced and tested?
  • Nudge economy: budget, cooldown, and forms — and can agents ever detect they're being nudged? Is the attribution loop (gods/intuition) tracked mechanically or left to emergence?
  • Cost at 20 souls: nightly consolidation + encounter dialogue + narrator — what cadence and model tiering keeps an evening of play affordable?
  • The miscast valve: when a persona is a dud, what recourse exists — social pressure, exile by vote, generational turnover? (Personas stay fixed; the question is what the society can do.)
  • Social-act schema: does the shared engine's structured intent cover conversation, or does the hybrid need a distinct schema for social acts (tell, promise, accuse, propose)?
